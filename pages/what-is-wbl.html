Program Foundations

What Is Work-Based Learning?

Work-Based Learning (WBL) is structured, supervised, standards-aligned career preparation that connects classroom instruction to real or realistic work settings. It is not simply students taking on jobs — it is a progression of learning experiences designed, documented, and supervised by the school in coordination with community and employer partners.

Course project note: This page describes general WBL concepts as proposed for the Culinary Pathway. It does not represent finalized Greece Central School District policy. Final procedures would be determined by district approval.

How the pieces connect

The proposed model links four components so that skills learned in one setting reinforce the next:

  • Classroom instruction — nutrition, menu planning, safety and sanitation, and professional communication.
  • Lab experience — hands-on kitchen practice under instructor supervision.
  • School-based enterprise — catering, pop-ups, and community events connected to the pathway.
  • Employer & community partnerships — supervised, structured experiences with local training partners.

Skills students may build

Technical skills

May include knife skills, food safety/sanitation, HACCP/FIFO practices, culinary math, menu planning, marketing, preparation of eggs/dairy/produce/grains/pasta/potatoes/legumes/poultry/meat/seafood, bakery production, sustainable practices, and food safety certification (e.g., ServSafe or similar).

Employability skills

May include attendance and punctuality, workplace appearance, quality of work, resource management, communication, critical thinking and problem solving, ethical and legal responsibilities, interpersonal dynamics, foundational workplace habits, and safety.

The school's role

Across all WBL experiences, the school is designed to maintain responsibility for communication between students, families, and partners; documentation of learning and hours; on-site or remote supervision as appropriate; safety protocols; and alignment of experiences to course and industry standards. Employers are envisioned as training partners who support student learning, not simply as job providers.

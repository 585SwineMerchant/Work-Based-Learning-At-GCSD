School-Based Enterprise: A Bridge Between Classroom Kitchen and Career

How catering, pop-ups, food sales, and student-run culinary experiences can help prepare students for Work-Based Learning.

School-Based Enterprise, or SBE, gives students the opportunity to apply culinary and employability skills in authentic projects while still being supported by teachers and school systems. In a culinary pathway, SBE may include catering requests, pop-up food events, food sales, community meals, internal service projects, or other student-supported foodservice experiences.

For a proposed Culinary Work-Based Learning program, school-based enterprise can serve as an important bridge. Students practice the expectations of the workplace — safety, sanitation, timing, communication, teamwork, customer service, quality control, and professionalism — before moving into more formal employer-connected experiences.

Course project note: Specific enterprise activities, schedules, and operating procedures shown here are illustrative concepts. Actual school-based enterprise offerings would be determined through district approval and program planning.

What Is School-Based Enterprise?

A school-based enterprise is a student-involved business or service experience connected to the curriculum. It is not just a fundraiser or extra activity. When designed intentionally, it becomes an instructional laboratory where students practice career skills, academic skills, and employability habits in a realistic setting.

In culinary education, SBE can make the classroom kitchen feel more like a professional operation while still keeping learning, reflection, and supervision at the center.

Skills Students Practice Through SBE

Technical and Business Skills

  • Food safety and sanitation
  • Mise en place and production planning
  • Knife skills and cooking/baking techniques
  • Recipe scaling and culinary math
  • Food and labor costing
  • Menu planning
  • Ordering, inventory, and resource management
  • Packaging, presentation, and service
  • Cleanup and station reset

Employability and Leadership Skills

  • Attendance and punctuality
  • Communication
  • Teamwork
  • Problem solving under pressure
  • Customer service
  • Professional appearance
  • Accepting feedback
  • Leadership and delegation
  • Ethical responsibility
  • Quality control and accountability

From SBE to WBL Readiness

  1. 1

    Classroom Foundation

    Students learn safety, sanitation, equipment use, and basic culinary techniques.

  2. 2

    Lab Practice

    Students practice skills through teacher-guided culinary labs.

  3. 3

    School-Based Enterprise

    Students apply skills in authentic school/community service experiences with real deadlines and expectations.

  4. 4

    Employer-Connected Experiences

    Students may interact with guest speakers, mentors, site visits, job shadows, or advisory partners.

  5. 5

    Formal WBL Opportunities

    When appropriate and approved, students may move toward internships, cooperative work experiences, or other supervised WBL models.

  6. 6

    Reflection and Evaluation

    Students review feedback, document growth, and connect the experience to career goals.

Why SBE Matters for Employers

SBE can help employers see that students are not starting from zero. Students may have practiced professional habits, customer service, production timelines, sanitation expectations, and teamwork before entering outside worksites.

Employers may benefit from students who have practiced:

  • Working on a team
  • Following production timelines
  • Communicating with supervisors and peers
  • Maintaining sanitation and safety expectations
  • Serving customers or clients
  • Receiving and applying feedback
  • Understanding quality standards

Community Connection

School-based enterprise can also strengthen the connection between the culinary pathway and the broader school/community. Catering, pop-ups, and community events can make student learning visible and help families, staff, employers, and community partners understand the value of the pathway.

SBE as Evidence of Learning

SBE creates evidence that can support student evaluation and program evaluation. Possible evidence may include:

  • Event planning documents
  • Menus and recipes
  • Production schedules
  • Costing sheets
  • Student role assignments
  • Customer feedback
  • Teacher observation notes
  • Student reflections
  • Photos or media, if approved
  • Employability skill evaluations
  • Safety/sanitation checklists

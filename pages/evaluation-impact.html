Evaluation & Impact: Measuring Student Growth and Program Quality

A proposed accountability framework for Culinary Work-Based Learning.

The proposed Greece Culinary Work-Based Learning program is designed to formalize, document, and expand student growth from both existing pathway strengths — such as ServSafe certification, in-house catering, and school-based enterprise — and any new, more structured WBL experiences. To understand whether the program is working, the school would need to collect meaningful evidence over time.

Evaluation should not be limited to whether students were placed at a worksite. A strong WBL evaluation system should examine readiness, participation, skill growth, employer feedback, student reflection, safety, access, and long-term career planning.

Course project note: No evaluation data currently exists for this proposed program. This page reflects the evaluation framework being developed as part of graduate coursework and will be updated as the Evaluation and Assessment Plan is completed.

Why Evaluation Matters

  • Evaluation helps improve the program.
  • Evaluation protects WBL from becoming unstructured or low-quality.
  • Evaluation documents student learning.
  • Evaluation helps employers give meaningful feedback.
  • Evaluation helps the district communicate impact to families, administrators, and the Board of Education.
  • Evaluation gives the culinary advisory board concrete evidence to guide advisory board-supported expansion of WBL structures.

Student Growth Indicators

Student growth may be measured through:

  • Readiness checklists
  • Employability profiles
  • Teacher observation
  • Employer evaluation
  • Reflection logs
  • Hours logs
  • Culinary skill demonstrations
  • Safety/sanitation performance
  • Resume/interview artifacts
  • Career planning reflections

Employer and Community Feedback

Employer input could help answer:

  • Are students prepared for professional expectations?
  • Which skills are strongest?
  • Which skills need more classroom/lab preparation?
  • Are communication and documentation systems clear?
  • Would the employer partner again?
  • What changes would strengthen the program?

Equity, Access, and Student Support

Evaluation should look at who is participating and whether students have fair access to preparation and opportunities. This may include:

  • Access across buildings or student groups
  • Transportation barriers
  • Student readiness supports
  • Family communication
  • Accommodations or student needs
  • Avoiding gatekeeping while still maintaining safety/readiness

Continuous Improvement Cycle

  1. 1

    Collect Evidence

    Gather data from readiness tools, logs, evaluations, and reflections.

  2. 2

    Review Patterns

    Look across the evidence for trends in growth, participation, and feedback.

  3. 3

    Identify Strengths and Gaps

    Determine what is working well and where students or partners need more support.

  4. 4

    Adjust Instruction and Supports

    Use findings to inform classroom instruction, readiness preparation, and program design.

  5. 5

    Communicate Results

    Share findings with students, families, employers, and school leadership.

  6. 6

    Plan Next Steps

    Set goals for the next cycle of program improvement.

Public Impact Summary

Future data could support:

  • Board of Education presentations
  • Employer recruitment
  • Family communication
  • Advisory committee planning
  • Student pathway improvement
  • CTE program quality evidence

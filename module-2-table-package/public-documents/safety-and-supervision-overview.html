Safety and Supervision Overview

Greece Culinary Work-Based Learning

Safety is intended to be a foundational part of every stage of the culinary pathway — not something added on only once a student leaves the building. This overview describes what is already in place in the classroom and lab, and how supervision is designed to extend to any future off-site experience.

Safety Already Built Into the Classroom & Kitchen Lab

Before any student is considered ready for a more formal WBL experience, the pathway already builds safety habits through real coursework and real production:

  • ServSafe food safety certification coursework and exam preparation
  • HACCP-informed practices for identifying and controlling food safety hazards
  • FIFO (first in, first out) stock rotation and proper food storage
  • Knife safety, equipment operation, and personal protective practices
  • Sanitation procedures for equipment, surfaces, and hands
  • Safe practices during real production and service for school and faculty events

How Supervision Works Today

Culinary instructors supervise students directly in the classroom and kitchen lab, including during school-based enterprise activities such as catering and in-house events. Students are introduced to safety expectations progressively across the five-course sequence — Strategies for Success, Culinary 1, Culinary 2, Advanced Culinary, and Kitchen Management — before taking on more independent or higher-responsibility roles.

How This Is Designed to Extend to Off-Site Experiences

For any future off-site WBL placement, the program is designed so the school retains responsibility for coordinating supervision — whether through direct instructor involvement, a program coordinator, or a formal agreement with an employer training partner. Employers would be expected to provide real, on-site supervision and to communicate with the school about student performance and any concerns.

Honest note: Specific off-site safety protocols, site-approval procedures, and supervision ratios have not yet been finalized. These would be established and approved by the district before any off-site placement begins.

Inclusive by Design

Because real production and service events involve many different roles — production, service, sanitation, marketing, costing, and reflection — the pathway is designed to support students with a range of strengths, needs, 504 plans, or IEPs through appropriate role assignment and supervision, not a one-size-fits-all placement.
